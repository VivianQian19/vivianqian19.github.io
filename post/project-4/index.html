Practice Lean Start-up as a UX Researcher

Discovering the importance of customer-centered research

PROJECTS

Practice Lean Start-up as a UX Researcher

March 10, 2021

Overview
swrm is a software start-up with the mission to help people live a more sustainable lifestyle using technology. The company has released an app in the app store. The app’s users are growing slowly and the company needed strategic recommendations on how to improve their existing app.

Goals
Initially, the cofounders wanted me to work on improving the existing app features. I provided a thorough heuristic analysis of the existing app using Nielsen’s 10 heuristic and pointed out several outstanding issues that needs to be fixed. But after having several meetings with swrm’s founders to understand their business’ value hypothesis, target customer base, I found that their previous research on value hypothesis validation asked many leading questions and was not rigorous. Since the app’s success depends on a valid value hypothesis, I recommended to the stakeholders the need to validate their value hypothesis first.

Method
I used a mixed-method approach to test swrm’s value hypotheses.

I designed an online survey using Google Forms to look at people’s sustainability habits and attitudes and used secondary research to break down the target population into two distinct segments with a screening question. I complemented the survey results with 10 in-depth 1:1 interviews to understand people’s motivation and desires in making transitions to a sustainable lifestyle and the problems that they may have encountered along that journey.

Impact
My research findings show that swrm’s value hypothesis was not in line with the problems their target consumers are having at the moment. I provided strategic recommendations on how they could pivot their consumer base so that they can find a market that is more profitable than what they have currently.

Reflection
Through this project experience, I have learned two things: 1) the importance of customer-centered research; 2) the importance of asking non-leading questions.

During the project, besides designing and executing the research project, I found myself spending extra amount of time evangelizing and convincing the stakeholders/ co-founders the importance of this project and customer-centric research. I spent a considerable amount of time trying to point the co-founders to the right directions and sometimes this process ended up in frustration. The co-founders at swrm used essentially a product development model that is not agile or lean because they fail to take into consideration of their customers’ needs, that is, they have not tested their value propositions with customer centered research before launching the product. Steve Bank’s Customer Development Manifesto sums this type of failure well for start-ups: “Startups don’t fail because they lack a product; they fail because they lack customers and a profitable business model”. As such, my main task as a UX researcher is to point them to the right direction by presenting them with my findings from surveys and from talking to their potential customers and to let the data show them the direction. It was hard because what I found is that the founders became so attached to their product and their own ideas of what would work, they overlooked whether their value propositions were relevant to their customers.

I have also learned the importance of asking non-leading questions. I have constantly referred to the Mom’s Test for reference on the best practices in terms of asking non-leading questions for customer discovery phase of interviews. I found this process to be iterative. During my interviews, I have iterated about 6 versions of interview questions. I learned some great tactics for testing product idea with customers. For instance, I try to keep the conversation as organic and natural as possible and never mention the exact service or product that the company is offering. I focus my questions on customers’ past behaviors instead of their opinions and refrain from asking hypothetical questions unless I follow up with a why question to understand the motivations of their choices.

swrm has also done research on its customers but I found they’ve asked a lot of leading questions during the interview (I read their interview scripts). For instance, they asked “usability testing” type of questions by asking directly their customers whether they would like to use their product if their product provides x features (which reminds me of this clip about Jared’s Survey from Silicon Valley). They also asked a lot of opinion-loaded questions or leading questions by directly tapping into people’s opinion of their potential product instead of asking people their pain points first and to see whether that is actually a problem.

But overall, I found this project experience rewarding. I have tested new ground working as a UX researcher in start-ups and discovered new understanding of entrepreneurship and its lean practices. The research experience I had and the challenges I overcame have informed me about the best practices as a UX researcher in drafting interview questions and ways to communicate the importance of customer-centered research to key stakeholders that I work with.
